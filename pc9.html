Structural Analysis of a Pilatus PC-9 Wing

Role: Structural Analysis and Composite-Design Contributor

This project used the Pilatus PC-9 wing as a case study for developing transferable structural-analysis skills. The work progressed from aerodynamic load identification to forces and moments, stress analysis, static failure assessment, fatigue, fracture mechanics, material selection and composite modernisation. The central outcome was a repeatable engineering methodology that can be applied to aerospace structures, mechanical assemblies, trusses and other load-bearing systems.

Engineering Problem and Analytical Approach

The Structural Problem

Aircraft wings are exposed to distributed aerodynamic forces that produce shear, bending and torsion throughout the internal structure. A safe design must withstand individual operational loads while also remaining resistant to fatigue, crack growth and repeated loading over its service life. The engineering challenge was therefore to convert aerodynamic loading into a defined structural stress state and assess the likely modes of failure.

My Analytical Approach

The wing loading was converted into forces and moments acting at a selected spar section. The resulting stresses were analysed before evaluating margins of safety, notched behaviour, fracture cases and fatigue performance. Material selection and composite redesign were then used to investigate how an existing metallic aerospace structure could be modernised through analytical engineering methods.

Static Structural Analysis

Static analysis established how aerodynamic loads were transferred into the wing spar and how the resulting internal forces generated structural stresses.

Load Definition

Spanwise Aerodynamic Force Distribution

The aerodynamic loading was represented using an elliptical spanwise lift distribution. This established how the applied force reduced from the wing root toward the tip and provided the basis for calculating an equivalent concentrated load and its effective location.

Engineering outcome: aerodynamic loading was translated into a defined structural load case suitable for force, moment and stress analysis.
Stress Interpretation

Idealised Bending Stress Distribution

Bending produces opposing tensile and compressive normal stresses across a structural section. The stress varies approximately linearly through the section height and passes through zero at the neutral axis. This principle applies to aircraft spars, beams, brackets, shelves, bridge members and many other load-bearing structures.

Engineering outcome: the analysis demonstrated how an applied bending moment can be converted into a predictable stress distribution across a structural section.
Static Failure

Structural Margin-of-Safety Assessment

Calculated stresses were compared against the allowable material strength at critical spar locations. The margin-of-safety comparison helped identify which location was governing the structural assessment while confirming that the analysed locations retained positive static safety margins.

Engineering outcome: Point B represented the lowest of the displayed static margins, while the web retained the greatest calculated reserve against the assessed failure condition.

Dynamic Failure and Structural Life

A structure may withstand a single static load while remaining vulnerable to fatigue and crack growth under repeated service loading. Dynamic assessment therefore considered safe-life, damage tolerance and fracture mechanics.

Fracture Mechanics

Comparison of Critical Crack Configurations

Three crack configurations were assessed: a crack emanating from a flange hole, a centre crack within the web and an edge crack along the flange. The analysis demonstrated that crack location and nearby geometry strongly influence local stress concentration, stress intensity and the resulting margin of safety.

Engineering outcome: the flange-hole case produced the lowest fracture margin of safety and was therefore the most critical of the three assessed configurations.

Safe-Life Assessment

Estimates the service period or number of load cycles that a structural component can withstand before fatigue becomes critical.

Damage Tolerance

Assumes that a defect may already exist and evaluates whether the structure can continue operating safely until inspection and repair.

Fracture Mechanics

Relates crack size, applied stress, geometry and fracture toughness to the likelihood of stable or unstable crack growth.

Material Selection and Composite Modernisation

The material investigation examined how modern composite materials could be used to improve an existing aerospace structure while maintaining its required strength and stiffness.

Material Engineering

Weight and Structural-Efficiency Comparison

The composite redesign was not treated as a simple material substitution. Directional composite properties, ply orientation, structural stiffness and load-bearing requirements had to be considered before an efficient configuration could be proposed.

Engineering outcome: the project developed experience in mathematically incorporating advanced materials as a modernisation method for an existing aerospace structure.

Why use composites?

Composite materials can provide high specific strength and stiffness while reducing structural weight. They can also improve corrosion resistance, but their performance depends on fibre orientation, laminate design, manufacturing quality and inspection requirements.

Why is this a modernisation exercise?

The starting point was an existing aluminium aircraft structure with established loads and structural requirements. The composite design therefore had to reproduce or improve the required performance rather than being designed without reference to the original system.

How does this connect to DFMA?

The same design approach was reflected in my aerospace bracket project, where structural performance was considered alongside manufacturing method, assembly, mass and practical component geometry.

How does this connect to the MiG-21 study?

My MiG-21 systems-design study similarly explored how an existing aircraft could be improved through the integration of contemporary engineering technologies. Both projects reflect an interest in modernising established aerospace systems through analytical design decisions.

Engineering Reflection

The most valuable outcome of this project was understanding how a structural problem can be solved systematically. The process begins by defining the loads and boundary conditions, followed by determining internal forces and moments, calculating stresses, identifying possible failure mechanisms and comparing the structural demand against the capacity of the selected material. Static analysis explains whether a component can withstand an applied load, while fatigue and fracture analysis explain whether it can remain safe under repeated operation and potential damage. The composite redesign extended this methodology into the modernisation of an existing aerospace structure, connecting structural analysis with material selection, manufacturing considerations and broader aircraft-system improvement.
