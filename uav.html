Autonomous UAV Behavioural Flight-Control Architecture

Role: Autonomous Systems & Behavioural-Control Developer

This project investigates how an autonomous UAV could navigate through mountainous terrain, maintain stable flight and alter its mission priorities when a predefined environmental hazard is detected. The prototype integrates flight dynamics, lateral and longitudinal control, map-based guidance, virtual sensing and an AI-inspired behavioural supervisor within one MATLAB and Simulink simulation environment.

Engineering Problem and Proposed Solution

The Operational Problem

Autonomous UAVs operating through mountainous terrain cannot rely only on basic waypoint following. Narrow flight corridors, terrain hazards, changing mission priorities and limited operator intervention require the aircraft to interpret its operating condition while continuing to maintain stable flight. A conventional controller can track altitude, speed and heading commands, but it does not independently decide when those commands should change.

My Proposed Solution

I developed a map-informed autonomous UAV architecture in which a behavioural supervisor monitors navigation errors, environmental disturbances and mission status before modifying altitude, speed and lateral-control priorities. The supervisory layer does not replace the flight controller. Instead, it changes the mission objective while the low-level controllers and flight dynamics determine the physically achievable aircraft response.

Engineering Objectives

Autonomous Valley Navigation

Follow a mapped valley route without continuous pilot steering.

Lateral Containment

Remain within a preferred ±250 m corridor and a maximum ±500 m safe corridor.

Altitude Regulation

Maintain a nominal mission altitude of approximately 100 m above ground level.

Environmental Response

Detect a predefined hazard region and change mission priorities accordingly.

Stable Aircraft Dynamics

Maintain bounded actuator, angle-of-attack, roll and yaw-rate responses.

Mission Completion

Recognise the end of the mapped route and transition into a completed state.

Integrated System Architecture

The prototype was divided into modular sensing, guidance, control, behavioural and aircraft-dynamics subsystems. This separation allowed each engineering function to be developed and evaluated independently before complete system integration.

Environment Map

Defines the valley centreline, corridor limits, directional guidance information and predefined disturbance locations.

Lateral Guidance Controller

Uses heading error and cross-track error to generate coordinated aileron and rudder commands for route following.

Longitudinal Controller

Regulates altitude and speed through elevator and throttle commands while responding to behavioural gain changes.

Behavioural Supervisor

Selects normal tracking, disturbance/climb priority, lateral recovery or mission-complete behaviour according to the interpreted operating condition.

Custom UAV Model

Represents the simplified three-degree-of-freedom aircraft response and translates control commands into aircraft position and motion states.

Virtual Sensor Pack

Estimates angle of attack, roll response and yaw rate and assigns a sensor status for supervisory monitoring.

Nominal Mission Validation

The baseline simulation was conducted without an active centreline disturbance. It established whether the behavioural additions could operate without degrading normal route-following and altitude-control performance.

Nominal Navigation

Autonomous Valley-Centreline Following

The UAV followed the general curvature of the mapped valley from the initial position to the final waypoint without direct pilot steering. The centreline was used as a guidance reference rather than a mandatory zero-error path, allowing the aircraft to demonstrate realistic bounded tracking behaviour.

Validation outcome: the UAV remained within the ±500 m safe corridor throughout the valid nominal mission and operated predominantly within the preferred ±250 m corridor.
Longitudinal Control

Nominal Altitude Hold

The longitudinal controller maintained the aircraft close to the scheduled mission altitude of 100 m above ground level throughout the baseline route. The close agreement between actual altitude and required altitude demonstrated that the behavioural layer did not degrade the underlying altitude-hold function.

Validation outcome: stable nominal altitude regulation with only limited elevator correction.
Aircraft Response

Control Commands and Virtual Sensors

Aileron and rudder commands changed direction according to the alternating curvature of the valley route, while throttle adjusted to support changes in manoeuvring demand. The estimated angle of attack, roll state and yaw rate remained bounded and returned toward zero after the final manoeuvring segment.

Validation outcome: finite actuator commands and controlled virtual-sensor responses throughout the nominal flight.
Supervisory Control

Tracking Errors and Behavioural Feedback

The supervisor alternated primarily between normal tracking and lateral recovery when additional heading or cross-track correction was required. Gain scaling changed according to the current navigation workload while preserving stable aircraft response.

Validation outcome: behavioural supervision supported route correction without destabilising the core guidance system.

Disturbance-Response Validation

A predefined centreline hazard was activated to determine whether the UAV could detect the changed mission condition, reprioritise its response and preserve controlled flight.

Hazard-Response Navigation

Valley Tracking During Disturbance

The lateral guidance controller remained active while the behavioural supervisor changed the longitudinal mission priorities. This allowed the UAV to continue following the mapped valley while initiating the climb-priority response.

Validation outcome: maximum cross-track error during the active disturbance was approximately 147.43 m, remaining inside the preferred ±250 m corridor.
Behavioural Priority Change

Emergency Altitude Response

When the hazard was detected, the behavioural supervisor increased the required altitude from 100 m to a deliberately large conceptual emergency command of 1000 m. The actual UAV climbed progressively rather than instantaneously because the low-level aircraft model remained constrained by elevator authority, throttle, forward speed, inertia and achievable climb rate.

Validation outcome: the UAV reached approximately 157.73 m during the active event and about 160.16 m over the full simulation, demonstrating separation between supervisory intent and physically achievable aircraft response.
Dynamic Stability

Control and Sensor Response

Control commands remained finite during disturbance entry, climb response and recovery. Elevator behaviour changed during the climb phase and subsequently reversed as the UAV returned toward the nominal altitude. Angle of attack, roll and yaw-rate estimates also remained bounded throughout the manoeuvre.

Validation outcome: maximum absolute aileron, elevator and rudder commands remained approximately 0.075, 0.030 and 0.080 respectively, while the virtual-sensor status remained normal.
AI-Inspired Supervision

Behavioural Controller Reprioritisation

During the active hazard interval, the behavioural controller increased the altitude-control gain, reduced the speed scale and retained sufficient lateral control authority. This demonstrates the intended AI-inspired behaviour: the system interpreted a mission condition and changed the relative priorities of the aircraft rather than merely responding to a single tracking error.

Validation outcome: the controller detected the hazard, entered climb-priority mode, reduced speed demand and preserved lateral containment until the disturbance cleared.

Engineering Design Questions

The following decisions explain why the prototype was structured in this form and how each architectural choice supports the engineering objective.

Why did I choose this architecture?

A modular architecture allows the environment map, guidance, flight control, behavioural logic, sensors and aircraft model to be developed and tested independently. It also provides clear interfaces for future upgrades such as improved sensors, new flight behaviours or higher-fidelity aircraft dynamics.

Why use a behavioural supervisor?

A flight controller determines how to track a command. A behavioural supervisor determines which command should be prioritised according to the interpreted mission condition. This separation allows the aircraft to retain stable low-level control while changing its operational objective.

Why split lateral and longitudinal control?

Lateral motion involves heading, roll, yaw and cross-track correction, while longitudinal motion involves altitude, speed, pitch, elevator and throttle. Separating these functions simplifies controller development and allows one axis to change priority without unnecessarily replacing the complete flight control system.

Why use virtual sensors?

Virtual sensors provide estimated aircraft-state information for development and supervisory monitoring before physical hardware is available. The concept allows angle of attack, roll and yaw-rate limits to be assessed within the simulation and prepares the architecture for future physical sensor integration.

How does the disturbance logic work?

The environment map identifies whether the aircraft has entered a predefined hazard region. Once active, the supervisor changes behaviour mode, raises the altitude objective, increases altitude priority and reduces speed demand. After the hazard clears, the nominal altitude command is restored and the aircraft gradually recovers according to its dynamics.

Why MATLAB and Simulink instead of Python?

MATLAB and Simulink allowed the aircraft model, control loops, decision logic and telemetry interfaces to be integrated within one time-based dynamic simulation. The graphical subsystem structure also supported signal tracing, controller development, logged-output analysis and future hardware-in-the-loop expansion.

Potential Real-World Applications

The current model is a conceptual prototype, but the underlying architecture could be adapted to several real aerospace missions requiring autonomous navigation and transparent behavioural decision-making.

Defence Reconnaissance

A map-informed UAV could support surveillance and reconnaissance through difficult terrain while reducing continuous operator workload. Behavioural control could prioritise altitude, speed or route containment when environmental or mission conditions change.

Emergency Medical Logistics

Autonomous UAVs could transport blood, medicines, vaccines or emergency equipment to remote communities and disaster-affected areas where terrain restricts conventional access. The behavioural layer could alter flight priorities in response to hazardous route conditions.

Forest and Environmental Services

The system could support bushfire observation, forest-health monitoring, wildlife surveys, illegal-logging detection and environmental mapping. Map-based corridor navigation could help maintain repeatable monitoring routes across remote regions.

AI, Autonomy and Engineering Ethics

Autonomous aerospace systems must be designed with safety, accountability and predictability as fundamental engineering requirements. The behavioural supervisor in this prototype uses transparent rule-based logic. Its decisions can therefore be inspected and traced to specific mission conditions, unlike an opaque system whose reasoning cannot easily be verified.

In real operations, autonomous behaviour should remain constrained by approved flight envelopes, validated mission rules and appropriate human oversight.

  • Safety-critical limits must override mission objectives.
  • Autonomous decisions should remain explainable and traceable.
  • Human operators must retain appropriate authority and accountability.
  • Sensor failures and uncertain data must be handled conservatively.
  • Defence and surveillance applications require proportionality, lawful use and clear rules of engagement.
  • Medical and civil applications require strong reliability, privacy and public trust.

Engineering Reflection

This project allowed me to connect flight dynamics, autonomous systems, behaviour-based AI and MATLAB/Simulink development within a single aerospace problem. The most important lesson was that autonomous intelligence should not replace the aircraft dynamics or low-level control system. Instead, it should modify mission priorities while allowing the aircraft model and flight controller to determine the physically achievable response. The project also strengthened my ability to move from an operational problem to a modular system architecture, create measurable requirements, test nominal and abnormal cases and communicate the engineering meaning of the results.
