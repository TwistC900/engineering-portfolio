Aircraft CAD Design & Engineering Drafting

Role: CAD Drafter & Aircraft Assembly Designer

This project involved reconstructing the complete T240 aircraft in CATIA V5 from supplied engineering drawings and assembly documentation. Individual structural components were interpreted from dimensioned drawings, modelled as parametric CAD parts and progressively integrated into the final aircraft assembly. The work demonstrates practical capability in aerospace drafting, engineering drawing interpretation, sheet-metal component modelling, constraint-based assembly and aircraft structural design.

Engineering Challenge

The Drafting Challenge

The aircraft was not supplied as a completed digital model. The design had to be reconstructed from an engineering drawing package containing aircraft assembly drawings, exploded wing layouts, component dimensions, airfoil coordinates, sheet-metal bend information, tolerances and structural interfaces. This required the design intent contained within two-dimensional drawings to be correctly interpreted before accurate three-dimensional components could be created.

The Completed Outcome

More than twenty individual CAD components were created and integrated into the complete aircraft. Representative parts included wing ribs, spars, channels, skins, control surfaces and structural mounting components. These were developed into wing, tail and complete-aircraft assemblies while maintaining alignment, symmetry and component compatibility.

Engineering Drawing Interpretation

The supplied drawing package formed the primary design input for the entire aircraft model. It included the complete T240 assembly, exploded wing structure, individual component drawings and detailed manufacturing information.

Developing the aircraft from this documentation required more than copying visible geometry. The drawings had to be interpreted as a connected engineering system in which each dimension, reference, bend and interface affected the final assembly.

  • Read third-angle projection drawings and detailed component views.
  • Interpreted linear and angular dimensions under ISO 2768-mK tolerances.
  • Used coordinate tables to recreate the specified airfoil profile.
  • Modelled sheet-metal thicknesses, flange geometry, bend radii and relief features.
  • Interpreted exploded assembly drawings and bills of materials.
  • Determined rib spacing, spar locations and component quantities.
  • Maintained the relationship between part drawings, wing assembly drawings and the complete aircraft assembly.
  • Converted drawing intent into editable three-dimensional parametric geometry.

Wing Skins and Control Surfaces

Part Modelling

Nose Rib Component

The nose rib was reconstructed from its individual manufacturing drawing, including its airfoil profile, lightening holes, mounting holes, flange geometry and bend details. Fourteen instances were required within the complete wing assembly.

CAD capability: drawing-to-model conversion, sheet-metal geometry, bend interpretation, dimension control and repeatable aerospace part creation.
Structural Component

Middle Rib Component

The middle rib was developed from detailed dimensional information defining the airfoil offset, flange positions, bend radius, fastener holes and weight-reduction openings. The completed component was then positioned repeatedly along the spar according to the wing assembly drawing.

CAD capability: manufacture-aware part modelling, geometric interpretation and accurate placement within a larger structural assembly.
Empennage Assembly

Tail Structure Integration

The tail assembly combines the fuselage structure with the horizontal and vertical stabiliser components. The assembly required alignment of the empennage relative to the aircraft centreline and verification of the interfaces between the structural members and rear fuselage.

CAD capability: empennage modelling, symmetry control, assembly positioning and structural interface verification.

Structural Design Development

After completing the baseline aircraft assembly from the supplied drawings, additional structural and aerodynamic refinements were developed directly within CATIA V5. These modifications focused on improving mounting rigidity, structural continuity, load-transfer paths and aerodynamic integration around the wing-fuselage interface.

Structural Reinforcement

Diagonal Strut Support Assembly

Diagonal support members were integrated between the lower fuselage mounting region and the upper wing-support structure. The triangulated arrangement introduced additional load paths and reduced the unsupported span of the structural members.

Engineering purpose: improved bending resistance, torsional rigidity, structural continuity and load transfer between the upper and lower support regions.
Wing Mount Design

Final Reinforced Head Bracket

The head bracket was developed as a reinforced interface between the aircraft wing and fuselage support structure. Rather than using a simple flat plate, the component incorporated an aerodynamic profile and internal reinforcement to support wing mounting while improving geometric continuity around the upper fuselage.

Engineering purpose: increased mounting rigidity, improved wing-load distribution and smoother aerodynamic transition around the wing root.
Assembly Integration

Wing Mount Configuration

The reinforced bracket was integrated into the complete aircraft using coincidence, contact, offset and centre-plane constraints. Its position was checked relative to the upper fuselage, wing support mounts, internal reinforcement and surrounding structural members.

CAD capability: designing new components within an existing assembly, managing interfaces and maintaining geometric compatibility during structural development.

Engineering Reflection

This project strengthened my ability to work from engineering documentation and translate two-dimensional design information into a complete digital aircraft. The most important skill developed was understanding that accurate CAD drafting requires more than reproducing visible geometry. Every component must be created with its dimensions, manufacturing details, interfaces and final assembly position in mind. Developing more than twenty parts and integrating them into wing, tail and complete-aircraft assemblies also strengthened my ability to manage complex CAD structures and verify component relationships. The additional structural modifications allowed me to progress from drawing interpretation into design development, where new components had to improve the assembly while remaining compatible with the existing aircraft geometry.
