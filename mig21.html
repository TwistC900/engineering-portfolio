Aerospace Systems Design

Modernisation and System Upgrade of the MiG-21 Bison

A preliminary design study developing an integrated avionics, propulsion and structural modernisation pathway for a legacy fighter platform, supported by requirements, performance trade studies and system-level concept selection.

02

Project Overview

The study defined a cost-effective modernisation pathway intended to extend the operational relevance of the MiG-21 Bison into the 2030s. It evaluated upgrades across three connected areas: radar and missile integration, propulsion, and airframe structure.

Performance trade studies, Breguet range analysis and requirement-based evaluation were used to identify a balanced integrated concept. The selected solution combined the Uttam AESA radar and Astra Mk-1 missile, a small afterburning turbofan, and targeted structural reinforcement with lightweight material substitution and fatigue-life extension measures.

03

Engineering Objectives

The revised requirements translated the modernisation problem into measurable capability, integration, performance and service-life objectives.

Beyond-Visual-Range Capability

Integrate a radar capable of at least 150 km detection range with an 80–100 km BVR missile engagement envelope.

Propulsion Efficiency

Reduce specific fuel consumption by at least 10% while retaining supersonic dash performance and improving thrust-to-weight ratio.

Structural Compatibility

Accommodate the radar and engine retrofit through forward-fuselage, rear-fuselage and engine-mount adaptations while managing mass distribution.

Life Extension and Survivability

Extend airframe life by approximately 2,000 flight hours and introduce practical radar-cross-section reduction and corrosion-control measures.

04

System Architecture

The architecture organised the aircraft into three primary subsystems: avionics and weapons, propulsion, and airframe and structure. Power, cooling and flight-control links were treated as the integration layer connecting these upgrades.

The Uttam AESA radar and Astra Mk-1 missile formed the combat-system core. A small afterburning turbofan was selected to improve fuel efficiency while preserving the interceptor role. Lightweight structural elements, reinforced mounts and fatigue-life measures supported the added systems and maintained aircraft compatibility.

05

Radar & Missile Trade Study

Radar–missile combinations were assessed using radar mass, missile mass, total installed mass, indicative detection range, cruise range and a composite score. The aim was to increase BVR effectiveness without creating unacceptable packaging, cooling or flight performance penalties.

The Uttam AESA and Astra Mk-1 combination achieved the highest composite score of 0.892. Its estimated 150 km radar detection range, 428 kg combined system mass and approximately 1,691 km cruise range provided the strongest balance of capability, integration feasibility and indigenous sustainment.

External stores governed endurance

Clean cruise range was approximately 1,876 km, reducing to about 1,691 km with two Astra Mk-1 missiles. The principal penalty came from store drag and weight.

Radar mass had limited range effect

Radar mass variation from 120 to 200 kg changed cruise range by only a few kilometres, leaving aerodynamic penalties as the stronger influence.

Aperture was constrained by integration

Larger radar apertures offered greater detection range, but the MiG-21 nose volume and cooling limits made the compact Uttam AESA the more feasible choice.

Selected configuration: Uttam AESA with Astra Mk-1. The pairing provided credible BVR capability with minimal relative range penalty while avoiding the packaging, cooling and supply-chain disadvantages identified in the alternatives.

06

Propulsion Trade Study

Four propulsion pathways were compared: sustaining and optimising the R-25-300, uprating the existing turbojet, installing a small afterburning turbofan, and adopting a higher-bypass non-afterburning turbofan.

The weighted study considered SFC improvement, mission-radius gain, thrust change, integration risk and mass increase. The small afterburning turbofan achieved the highest composite score of 0.78 and represented the best long-term balance between efficiency, thrust and compatibility with the MiG-21’s supersonic interceptor role.

Efficiency dominated mass penalty

Moderate avionics and missile mass increases up to 200 kg had limited endurance effect, whereas improved propulsion efficiency produced substantial range gains.

Small turbofan delivered the balance

The small afterburning turbofan reduced SFC by approximately 12%, improved mission radius by roughly 13–15%, and increased thrust by approximately 15%.

Maximum endurance was not the only criterion

The higher-bypass concept exceeded 3,000 km in the analysis, but its packaging, cooling and speed compromises conflicted with the aircraft’s interceptor role.

Selected propulsion concept: a small afterburning turbofan. Breguet-range analysis showed an improvement of roughly 350 km over the reference configuration while retaining the supersonic capability required by the design objectives.

07

Structural Modernisation

Structural changes were limited to targeted adaptations supporting system integration, mass control, durability and survivability without replacing the aircraft’s core layout.

  1. Lightweight Material Substitution

    Composite or aluminium-lithium materials were proposed for selected non-primary structures, including access panels and avionics-bay housings, to offset added system mass.

  2. Forward Fuselage and Radar Bay

    The radar bay was re-profiled and stiffened to accommodate the AESA installation and thermal loads while managing the associated forward centre-of-gravity shift.

  3. Engine Mount and Rear Fuselage Strengthening

    Reinforced mounts and bulkheads were required to support the different thrust, mass and vibration characteristics of the turbofan retrofit.

  4. Fatigue Life and Corrosion Control

    Cold expansion of fastener holes, composite patch repairs and corrosion-resistant coatings were proposed to extend fatigue life by approximately 1,500–2,000 hours.

  5. Radar-Cross-Section Reduction

    Limited contour reshaping and radar-absorbing coatings were included as practical survivability measures with negligible relative weight penalty.

08

Final Design Selection

Final concept selection combined the strongest subsystem outcomes into one integrated aircraft configuration. The selection was not based on the maximum performance of any single component; it was based on the best total balance of capability, efficiency, structural compatibility, survivability and requirement compliance.

Avionics & Weapons Uttam AESA radar with Astra Mk-1 missiles
Propulsion Small afterburning turbofan with digital control
Structure Lightweight substitution, reinforcement and life extension
09

Engineering Impact

The project demonstrates how a legacy platform can be evaluated through coordinated subsystem improvements while retaining focus on the operational role and integration limits of the original aircraft.

Capability with Controlled Penalties

The radar and missile selection improved BVR capability while the trade study kept aerodynamic, mass, cooling and packaging penalties visible in the decision.

Performance Driven by the Governing Variable

Sensitivity analysis showed that propulsion efficiency had a greater influence on endurance than moderate equipment mass growth, directing effort toward the more valuable design change.

Integrated Rather Than Isolated Upgrades

Radar, engine, cooling, power, mass distribution, structural reinforcement and flight control augmentation were treated as interacting parts of one aircraft-level solution.

Requirement Traceability

The final concept was selected against explicit BVR, SFC, thrust-to-weight, fatigue-life, survivability and compatibility objectives rather than through qualitative preference alone.

10

Engineering Skills Demonstrated

The work connected quantitative performance assessment with the broader decisions required when modifying an existing aerospace platform.

Systems Architecture

Decomposed the aircraft into avionics, weapons, propulsion and structural subsystems and identified the power, cooling, control and mass-distribution interfaces between them.

Quantitative Trade Studies

Compared competing radar, missile and propulsion concepts using performance, mass, integration risk and weighted composite criteria.

Performance and Sensitivity Analysis

Used Breguet-range modelling and sensitivity studies to separate the effects of SFC, lift-to-drag ratio, external stores and added system mass.

Requirement-Led Decision Making

Converted broad modernisation aims into measurable subsystem requirements and used those requirements to justify the integrated final concept.

11

Reflection

This project reinforced that aircraft modernisation is not a search for the individually highest-performing radar, engine or material. Each improvement changes the demands placed on other parts of the aircraft. A larger radar affects packaging, cooling and centre of gravity; a more efficient engine changes mounts, rear-fuselage loads and control integration; and structural reinforcement can preserve safety while eroding the mass benefit it was meant to protect. The final concept therefore emerged from balancing interfaces and sensitivities, not from maximising a single metric. That systems-level view was the most important outcome of the study.
