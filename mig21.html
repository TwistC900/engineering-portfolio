Aerospace Systems Design Project

Modernisation and System Upgrade of the MiG-21 Bison for Indian Airforce

Role: Aircraft Systems Design and Trade-Study Developer

This project investigated a cost-effective modernisation pathway for the Indian Air Force MiG-21 Bison through integrated radar, missile, propulsion and structural upgrades. The study examined how the aircraft's operational relevance could be extended through indigenous avionics and weapons integration, improved propulsion efficiency, structural-life extension and requirement-based concept selection. System architecture development, Breguet range analysis, quantitative trade studies and sensitivity analysis were used to identify a balanced final configuration while respecting the limitations of the existing airframe.

Engineering Problem and Proposed Solution

The Engineering Problem

The Indian Air Force MiG-21 Bison remained constrained by an ageing radar, limited situational awareness, propulsion inefficiency and structural fatigue. Improving only one subsystem would not resolve the aircraft-level problem because each proposed upgrade affected mass distribution, cooling, electrical power, aerodynamic drag, structural loads and overall mission performance. The engineering challenge was therefore to identify a modernisation package that could extend the aircraft's operational relevance without creating integration penalties that outweighed the benefit

My Proposed Solution

I developed an integrated concept combining the Uttam AESA radar and Astra Mk-1 missile, a small afterburning turbofan, lightweight material substitution, targeted structural reinforcement and fatigue-life extension measures. The concept was selected using quantitative comparison rather than preference alone. Radar and missile combinations were assessed against mass, range, capability and integration constraints, while propulsion concepts were assessed against fuel efficiency, mission radius, thrust, mass and implementation risk.

Engineering Objectives

Beyond-Visual-Range Capability

Integrate a radar with at least 150 km detection range and support an 80–100 km BVR missile engagement envelope.

Propulsion Efficiency

Reduce specific fuel consumption by at least 10% while maintaining the supersonic dash capability required by the interceptor role.

Improved Thrust-to-Weight Ratio

Achieve a minimum 12% improvement in thrust-to-weight ratio to support climb performance and manoeuvrability.

Mass Reduction

Use lightweight materials in selected secondary structures to reduce empty mass by approximately 150–200 kg.

Structural Life Extension

Introduce reinforcement, fatigue-management and corrosion-control measures to extend airframe life by approximately 2,000 flight hours.

Integrated Survivability

Improve defensive capability through modern sensors, electronic systems and practical radar-cross-section reduction measures.

Integrated System Architecture

The final architecture linked the aircraft's sensing, weapons, propulsion, structure, survivability, flight-control, power and cooling functions. This approach ensured that each subsystem upgrade was assessed as part of the whole aircraft rather than as an isolated replacement.

Uttam AESA Radar

Provides the primary sensor upgrade with an estimated 150 km detection range while remaining more compatible with the MiG-21 nose volume and cooling limits than the larger alternatives considered.

Astra Mk-1 Missile

Provides the selected indigenous BVR weapon capability with an engagement envelope of approximately 80–100 km and supports the self-reliance objective of the design.

Afterburning Turbofan

Replaces the R-25-300 turbojet conceptually with a more fuel-efficient propulsion system while retaining the afterburning capability required for supersonic interception.

Structural Adaptation

Includes radar-bay stiffening, engine-mount and rear-fuselage reinforcement, lightweight secondary structures and fatigue-life extension measures.

Power and Cooling

Supports the increased electrical and thermal demands created by the AESA radar, upgraded avionics and propulsion-system integration.

Flight-Control Augmentation

Compensates for changes in aircraft mass distribution created by the forward radar installation and the rear propulsion-system modification.

Radar and Missile Trade Study

The radar and missile evaluation combined performance calculations with mass, aerodynamic, packaging and sensor-capability considerations. The objective was to improve BVR effectiveness while keeping aircraft-performance penalties within acceptable limits.

Concept Comparison

Radar and Missile Configuration Evaluation

Candidate combinations were compared using radar mass, missile mass, total installed mass, indicative detection range, estimated cruise range and a weighted composite score. The Uttam AESA and Astra Mk-1 combination achieved the strongest overall balance between capability, mass, integration feasibility and indigenous support.

Selection outcome: the Uttam AESA and Astra Mk-1 combination achieved the highest composite score of 0.892, with an estimated 150 km radar range and approximately 1,691 km cruise range in the selected weapon configuration.
Performance Analysis

Range, Aperture and Sensitivity Results

The clean-aircraft cruise range was approximately 1,876 km, reducing to about 1,691 km with two Astra Mk-1 missiles. This indicated that external-store drag and weight had a greater effect on endurance than moderate radar-mass variation. Aperture scaling showed that larger radars could provide greater detection range, but the MiG-21's limited nose volume and cooling capacity reduced their integration feasibility.

Engineering conclusion: radar mass changes between approximately 120 and 200 kg altered range by only a few kilometres, while aerodynamic penalties from external stores governed the larger endurance loss.

Propulsion Trade Study

Four propulsion pathways were compared: optimisation of the existing R-25-300, an uprated turbojet, a small afterburning turbofan and a higher-bypass non-afterburning turbofan.

Propulsion Selection

Weighted Engine-Concept Evaluation

Each propulsion option was assessed using specific-fuel-consumption improvement, mission-radius gain, thrust change, integration risk, mass increase and a weighted composite score. The small afterburning turbofan did not provide the maximum range of every concept, but it delivered the strongest balance between efficiency, thrust and compatibility with the aircraft's supersonic interceptor role.

Selection outcome: the small afterburning turbofan achieved the highest composite score of 0.78, with approximately 12% lower SFC, 13–15% mission-radius improvement and about 15% greater thrust.
Range and Sensitivity

Effect of Propulsion Efficiency and Added Mass

The Breguet-based analysis showed that propulsion efficiency had a stronger influence on mission range than moderate avionics and weapon mass increases. Although a higher-bypass concept produced the greatest theoretical endurance, its packaging, cooling and speed compromises were inconsistent with the interceptor mission. The selected afterburning turbofan instead provided a practical long-term improvement while retaining supersonic capability.

Engineering conclusion: the selected propulsion concept improved estimated range by approximately 350 km over the reference configuration, demonstrating that SFC reduction was the governing performance benefit.

Structural Modernisation

The structural proposal focused on targeted changes required to carry the new systems, control mass growth, preserve durability and extend service life without replacing the aircraft's fundamental configuration.

Lightweight Material Substitution

Composites or aluminium-lithium alloys were proposed for selected non-primary structures, including access panels and avionics-bay housings, to offset added system mass.

Forward Fuselage Reinforcement

The radar bay required re-profiling and local stiffening to support the AESA installation, thermal loading and the resulting forward centre-of-gravity change.

Engine-Mount Strengthening

Reinforced engine mounts, bulkheads and rear-fuselage structure were required to support the different mass, thrust and vibration characteristics of the turbofan retrofit.

Fatigue-Life Extension

Cold expansion of fastener holes, composite patch repair and other targeted life-extension measures were proposed to increase fatigue life by approximately 2,000 flight hours.

Corrosion Control

Corrosion-resistant coatings and focused maintenance measures were included to support the continued operation of the ageing airframe.

Radar-Cross-Section Reduction

Minor contour reshaping and radar-absorbing coatings were proposed as practical survivability improvements with limited additional mass.

Final Design Selection

The final concept combined the strongest subsystem outcomes into one aircraft configuration. Selection was based on the best total balance of capability, efficiency, structural compatibility, survivability and requirement compliance rather than the maximum value of any single metric.

Avionics and Weapons

Uttam AESA radar, Astra Mk-1 missiles, helmet-mounted sighting and secure datalink capability formed the selected combat-system package.

Propulsion

A small afterburning turbofan was selected to improve fuel efficiency, mission radius and thrust while retaining supersonic dash capability.

Structure and Survivability

Lightweight substitution, reinforcement, fatigue-life extension, corrosion control and practical RCS-reduction measures supported the complete integration.

Engineering Design Questions

The following decisions explain why the final configuration was selected and how the trade studies informed the overall aircraft design.

Why was Uttam AESA selected?

Larger radar options offered greater theoretical detection range, but their mass, aperture, cooling and nose-volume requirements reduced their feasibility. Uttam provided the required 150 km class detection capability with a better integration balance.

Why pair it with Astra Mk-1?

The Astra Mk-1 provided the required BVR engagement capability while supporting the project's self-reliance objective. Its range penalty remained comparable with the other missile options evaluated.

Why choose a small afterburning turbofan?

The small turbofan improved SFC, mission radius and thrust while preserving the afterburning and supersonic performance needed for the interceptor role. It therefore offered a stronger operational balance than the maximum-endurance alternative.

Why not maximise range alone?

The highest-range propulsion concept introduced packaging, cooling and speed compromises that conflicted with the aircraft's intended role. System selection therefore required mission compatibility rather than single-variable optimisation.

Why include structural changes in a systems study?

New radar and propulsion systems change mass distribution, local loading, thermal demand and vibration. Structural adaptation was therefore essential to make the avionics and propulsion selections physically credible.

What governed the final concept?

The governing principle was balanced requirement compliance. Capability, efficiency, mass, structural durability, survivability and integration risk were considered together before the final configuration was selected.

Engineering Reflection

This project strengthened my understanding that aircraft modernisation is not a search for the individually highest-performing radar, engine or material. Each proposed improvement changes the demands placed on other parts of the aircraft. A larger radar affects packaging, cooling and centre of gravity. A different engine changes structural loads, rear-fuselage integration and flight-control requirements. Structural reinforcement may preserve safety but can also reduce the mass benefit achieved elsewhere. The most important outcome was therefore learning to evaluate the aircraft as one connected system. The final concept emerged from balancing interfaces, performance sensitivities and mission requirements rather than maximising a single number.
